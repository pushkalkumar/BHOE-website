Energy to Matter

In the first fractions of a second, the universe was hot enough that energy condensed directly into matter. Einstein's equation E = mc² describes this: mass and energy are two forms of the same thing, and the relationship runs both ways.

E = mc²

One kilogram of matter contains roughly 9×10¹⁶ joules. That number is why nuclear reactions release so much energy from so little mass.

The Plasma State

For the first 380,000 years, the universe was too hot for atoms to exist. Thermal energy exceeded the ionization energies of hydrogen and helium, so electrons couldn't stay bound to nuclei. Matter was a fog of charged particles, and the universe was completely opaque because free electrons scattered light in every direction.

In plasma, kT is much greater than IE, so electrons roam freely.

Free protons and electrons in plasma

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

The Big Bang produced hydrogen, helium, and a trace of lithium. That is it. Every other element on the periodic table was made later, inside stars. When Bryson writes that we are made of stardust, he means it literally.

All elements heavier than lithium were forged in stars. We are, in a very real sense, made of stellar ash.

AP Chemistry Connection · Unit 1: Atomic Structure

The Recombination event is ionization energy in reverse. As the universe cooled past roughly 3,000 K, thermal energy dropped below the ionization energies of hydrogen (13.6 eV) and helium (24.6 eV). Electrons settled into ground-state orbitals for the first time. Those were the first electron configurations in history, and the universe became transparent the moment they formed.

Stars as Nuclear Reactors

How stellar fusion creates every element heavier than lithium. Unit 1: Nuclear Chemistry · Big Idea: Energy and Transformations

The Proton-Proton Chain

In stars like the Sun, hydrogen fuses into helium through the proton-proton chain. It requires temperatures above about 10 million K because protons must get close enough for the strong nuclear force to overcome their electrostatic repulsion.

¹H+¹H →²H+ e⁺+ν
Step 1: Deuterium forms
↓
²H+¹H →³He+γ
Step 2: Helium-3 forms
↓
³He+³He →⁴He+2¹H
Step 3: Helium-4 plus energy released

Net: 4 protons become one helium-4 nucleus plus 2 positrons, 2 neutrinos, 2 gamma rays, and 26.7 MeV of energy.

Binding Energy & the Iron Peak

Each fusion step releases energy because the product nucleus is slightly lighter than its reactants. That missing mass becomes energy through E = mc². Binding energy per nucleon peaks at iron-56, which is why iron is the most stable nucleus in the universe. Past iron, fusion no longer releases energy, it requires it.

Binding energy per nucleon (MeV) vs. mass number. Iron-56 sits at the top of the curve.

AP Chemistry Connection · Nuclear Chemistry

Fusion below iron and fission above iron both release energy for the same reason: both sides of the iron-56 peak slide toward higher binding energy per nucleon. In both cases, mass disappears, and that mass becomes energy through ΔE = Δmc².

Scientific Practice 1 · Models and Representations

The Atomic Model Timeline

How experimental evidence forced five revisions of the atomic model across 123 years

Solid Sphere
Dalton's Atomic Theory1803

Dalton built his model from patterns in chemical reactions, not from any observation of atoms directly. He noticed that elements always combine in fixed mass ratios, which makes mathematical sense if each element has its own type of particle with a fixed mass. It was more an accounting framework than a physical description.

Key Evidence

  • Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1774)
  • Law of Definite Proportions (Proust, 1799)
  • Law of Multiple Proportions

Limitation

The model had no mechanism for electrical phenomena, like why dissolved salts conduct electricity. The atom being truly indivisible turned out to be wrong, though it was a reasonable assumption for 1803.

Plum Pudding
Thomson's Plum Pudding Model1897

Thomson ran electricity through sealed glass tubes and found something odd: a beam that deflected identically regardless of what gas was inside or what the electrodes were made from. Whatever was in that beam came from the atoms themselves. The particles were far lighter than hydrogen, so atoms had to contain smaller pieces.

Key Evidence

  • Cathode rays deflected identically regardless of electrode material or gas used
  • e/m ratio: these particles have mass roughly 1/1837 that of hydrogen
  • Since atoms are electrically neutral, positive charge had to be somewhere

Limitation

Thomson assumed positive charge was spread thinly throughout the atom. That predicted heavy particles should drift through with barely any deflection. Rutherford fired those particles at gold foil and found out how wrong that was.

Nuclear Model
Rutherford's Nuclear Model1911

Geiger and Marsden fired alpha particles at gold foil in 1909. About 99.98% went straight through. But roughly one in 8,000 bounced back at steep angles. Rutherford later wrote that it was like firing artillery shells at tissue paper and having them come back at you. The only explanation was that most of the atom's mass was packed into a tiny, dense, positively charged core.

Key Evidence

  • ~99.98% of alpha particles passed through without significant deflection
  • ~1 in 8,000 bounced back at large angles, requiring a dense positive core
  • Nuclear radius ~10⁻¹⁵ m vs. atomic radius ~10⁻¹⁰ m (100,000 times smaller)

Limitation

Classical electromagnetism says any accelerating charge radiates energy continuously. An orbiting electron is always accelerating, so it should lose energy and spiral into the nucleus in roughly 10⁻⁸ seconds. Rutherford's model had no explanation for why atoms are stable.

Planetary / Bohr Model
Bohr's Planetary Model1913

Bohr took Planck's idea that energy comes in discrete chunks and applied it to atomic structure. He proposed that electrons can only orbit at specific allowed distances from the nucleus, and only radiate when jumping between those levels. This gave ΔE = hν a physical interpretation, and it matched hydrogen's emission spectrum exactly.

Key Evidence

  • Hydrogen's emission spectrum: four visible lines at 656, 486, 434, and 410 nm
  • Bohr's formula predicted all four frequencies with high accuracy
  • The model finally explained why atoms don't collapse

Limitation

The formula worked perfectly for hydrogen and failed immediately for helium. For a model of atomic structure, failing with two electrons was a serious problem.

Quantum Mechanical Model
Quantum Mechanical Model1926

Schrodinger's wave equation treats electrons not as particles in defined orbits but as probability distributions spread through space. The wave function squared gives the probability of finding an electron at a given point. Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle sets a hard limit: you cannot simultaneously know a particle's exact position and momentum. The more precisely you pin down one, the less you can know about the other.

Key Evidence

  • De Broglie (1924) predicted electrons have wavelength; electron diffraction confirmed this
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2
  • Quantum numbers (n, l, mₗ, mₛ) account for all observable electron properties

Current Understanding

This is still the model we use. It accounts for all atomic spectra, chemical bonding, and periodic trends. There is no experimental result at atomic scales that breaks it.

AP Chemistry Connection · Scientific Practice 1

None of these models got replaced because a better idea came along philosophically. Dalton's broke when cathode rays showed atoms had internal structure. Thomson's broke when Rutherford's gold foil found concentrated charge. Rutherford's broke because classical physics predicted atoms should collapse in nanoseconds. Bohr's broke because it only worked for one element. SP-1 in practice means models change when the data forces them to, not before.

Radioactive Decay

Nuclear instability, exponential decay, and the mathematics of half-life. Unit 1: Nuclear Chemistry

α

Alpha Decay

²³⁸₉₂U→ ²³⁴₉₀Th+⁴₂He
  • Emitted: helium-4 nucleus (2 protons, 2 neutrons)
  • Mass number drops by 4
  • Atomic number drops by 2
  • Penetrating power: low (stopped by paper)
  • Ionizing power: very high

The main decay mode for heavy nuclei above bismuth. Uranium and radium both decay this way.

β

Beta Decay (β⁻)

¹⁴₆C→ ¹⁴₇N+ e⁻+v̄ₑ
  • A neutron converts to a proton, emitting an electron and antineutrino
  • Mass number unchanged
  • Atomic number increases by 1
  • Penetrating power: medium (stopped by aluminum foil)
  • Ionizing power: moderate

Carbon-14 decays this way. The predictable rate is what makes radiocarbon dating work for samples up to about 50,000 years old.

γ

Gamma Emission

⁶⁰₂₇Co*→ ⁶⁰₂₇Co+γ
  • Emitted: high-energy photon
  • Mass and atomic number both unchanged
  • Penetrating power: extreme (needs lead or concrete)
  • Ionizing power: low (indirect)

Often accompanies alpha or beta decay when the daughter nucleus is left in an excited state. Used in medical imaging and cancer treatment.

AP Chemistry Connection · Scientific Practice 3

The half-life equation has one property worth noting: the rate of decay depends only on how much material you have right now, not on how old it is. A freshly formed carbon-14 atom and one that has been around for a thousand years decay with the same probability per unit time. That is first-order kinetics, which comes back in Unit 5 when you study reaction rates.

States of Matter in Space

Space has matter in all four states of aggregation, depending on local temperature and pressure.

PLASMA
Stellar interiors, solar wind
Temperatures above ~10⁶ K strip electrons from nuclei
GAS
Nebulae, planetary atmospheres
H, He, and molecules like CO and H₂O exist as gases in interstellar space

Subsurface oceans (Europa, Enceladus)

Planets, asteroids, neutron stars

Intermolecular Forces

Molecular structure determines bulk physical properties through the forces molecules exert on each other.

London DispersionWeakest

Instantaneous dipoles from electron fluctuations. Present in all substances. Strength increases with molecular size and polarizability.

All molecules; strength increases with molar mass
Dipole-DipoleModerate

Permanent partial charges on polar molecules attract each other. Added to London dispersion in polar substances like HCl and SO₂.

Polar molecules (e.g., HCl, SO₂)
Hydrogen BondingStrongest IMF

Hydrogen bonded to N, O, or F forms an unusually strong interaction, roughly 10 times stronger than typical dipole-dipole. Responsible for water's anomalously high boiling point and for DNA base pairing.

H-N, H-O, H-F bonds

Why Water is Strange

Based on molar mass alone, water should boil around -80°C. H₂S, the next compound in the same group, boils at -60°C. Water boils 160 degrees higher than the trend predicts. Every organism on Earth exists partly because of that gap, which comes entirely from the O-H hydrogen bonds water forms with itself. It also explains why ice floats, which turns out to matter a great deal for aquatic life.

AP Chemistry Connection · Units 3 and 5

Europa might have a liquid water ocean under its ice because water's IMFs allow it to stay liquid at temperatures where other compounds would freeze. Titan has liquid methane oceans because methane's weaker London forces give it a much lower boiling point. The same equations from your phase change unit end up determining which moons are geologically active.
