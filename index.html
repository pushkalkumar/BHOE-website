Energy to Matter: E = mc²

In the first fractions of a second, the universe was hot enough that pure energy condensed into matter. Einstein's equation describes this conversion: mass and energy are the same thing expressed differently. The relationship runs in both directions.

E = mc²

One kilogram of matter stores roughly 9 × 1016 joules. That figure, the speed of light squared, is why nuclear reactions release so much energy from so little mass. The same conversion ran in reverse in the first second of the universe, turning pure radiation into the first protons and neutrons.

The mass of a proton is 1.673 × 10−27 kg. Its rest energy is 938 MeV. Every time a nuclear reaction releases energy, that mass difference is accounted for by Einstein's equation.

The Plasma State

For the first 380,000 years, the universe was too hot for atoms to exist. Thermal energy far exceeded the ionization energies of hydrogen (13.6 eV) and helium (24.6 eV), so electrons could never stay bound to nuclei. The universe was a fog of charged particles: protons, neutrons, and free electrons. Light scattered off every electron, making the cosmos completely opaque.

Free protons (●) and electrons (●) in plasma

kT ≫ IE means thermal energy far exceeds ionization energy. The same condition applies inside stars today, where core temperatures exceed 10 million Kelvin and all matter is fully ionized plasma.

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis

The Big Bang produced three stable elements: hydrogen, helium, and a trace of lithium. Everything else on the periodic table was assembled later, inside stars. When Bryson says we are made of stardust, he means it literally.

Stars as Nuclear Reactors

Every element heavier than helium was built inside a star. The silicon in your phone, the oxygen in the air, the iron in your blood: all of it was forged in stellar cores and scattered across the galaxy when those stars died.

The Proton-Proton Chain

In stars like the Sun, hydrogen fuses into helium through the proton-proton chain. Temperatures must exceed ~10 million K so protons can overcome their electrostatic repulsion and get close enough for the strong nuclear force to bind them.

¹H+¹H →²H+ e⁺+νe
Step 1 · Deuterium forms; positron and neutrino emitted
↓
²H+¹H →³He+γ
Step 2 · Helium-3 forms; gamma ray photon emitted
↓
³He+³He →⁴He+2¹H
Step 3 · Helium-4 forms; two protons returned; energy released

Net reaction: 4 ¹H → ¹ ⁴He + 2e⁺ + 2νe + 2γ + 26.73 MeV. The 26.73 MeV comes entirely from the mass difference: four proton masses minus one helium-4 mass, converted to energy via E = mc².

Binding Energy & the Iron Peak

Fusion reactions release energy because the product nucleus is slightly lighter than its ingredients. That missing mass becomes energy through ΔE = Δmc². Binding energy per nucleon peaks at iron-56. Below iron, fusion releases energy; above iron, it requires it. That's why iron marks the endpoint of stellar fusion, and why iron cores trigger supernovae.

The Atomic Model Timeline

How five experiments over 123 years forced five revisions of the atomic model. Each model was replaced not by a better idea but by data that broke the previous one.

Indivisible Solid Sphere
Dalton's Atomic Theory 1803

Dalton built his model entirely from patterns in chemical reactions, never having seen an atom. He observed that elements always combine in fixed mass ratios (water is always 1 part hydrogen to 8 parts oxygen by mass) and concluded that the simplest explanation was discrete particles with fixed masses. Each element had its own type of atom; compounds were fixed combinations.

Key Evidence

  • Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1774): mass is neither created nor destroyed
  • Law of Definite Proportions (Proust, 1799): compounds always have fixed elemental ratios
  • Law of Multiple Proportions: when elements form multiple compounds, mass ratios are simple integers

Why It Was Replaced

The solid-sphere model had no mechanism for electrical phenomena, like why dissolved salts conduct electricity. It also implied atoms were truly indivisible. When Thomson ran electricity through sealed tubes and found particles 1/1837 the mass of hydrogen emerging regardless of electrode material, the indivisible atom was finished.

Plum Pudding Model
Thomson's Plum Pudding Model 1897

Thomson connected a high-voltage source to sealed glass tubes and found a ray coming from the negative electrode. The key observation was that the ray deflected identically regardless of what gas filled the tube or what metal the cathode was made from. Whatever was in that beam came from matter itself, not from any particular material. Thomson measured the charge-to-mass ratio and found these particles were roughly 1,837 times lighter than hydrogen. The atom had internal structure.

Key Evidence

  • Cathode rays deflect in electric and magnetic fields identically regardless of electrode material
  • e/m ratio measurements: these particles are ~1/1837 the mass of hydrogen atoms
  • Since atoms are electrically neutral, the positive charge had to be distributed somewhere in the atom
  • Millikan (1909) later measured the electron's actual charge: 1.602 × 10−19 C

Why It Was Replaced

If positive charge is spread uniformly through the atom, heavy alpha particles should drift through gold foil with barely any deflection, like cannonballs through a fog. Rutherford fired those particles and watched one in 8,000 bounce straight back. A diffuse sphere can't do that.

Nuclear Model
Rutherford's Nuclear Model 1911

Geiger and Marsden directed a beam of alpha particles at a gold foil 400 atoms thick. About 99.98% passed straight through. But roughly 1 in 8,000 bounced back at angles greater than 90 degrees. Rutherford later said it was like firing artillery shells at tissue paper and having them return at you. The only explanation: nearly all the atom's mass is concentrated in a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus, and the rest is empty space.

Key Evidence

  • ~99.98% of alpha particles pass straight through with barely any deflection, meaning the atom is mostly empty
  • ~1 in 8,000 bounce back at large angles, which only makes sense if positive charge is concentrated in a tiny, dense core
  • Nuclear radius ~10−15 m vs. atomic radius ~10−10 m: the nucleus occupies 10−15 of the atom's volume
  • Chadwick (1932) later discovered neutrons complete the nuclear picture

Why It Was Replaced

Classical electromagnetism states that any accelerating charge radiates energy continuously. An orbiting electron is constantly accelerating toward the nucleus. It should lose energy and spiral inward, crashing into the nucleus in roughly 10−8 seconds. Rutherford's model predicted atoms should be spectacularly unstable. They are not.

Quantized Planetary Model
Bohr's Planetary Model 1913

Bohr took Planck's idea that energy only comes in discrete packets and applied it to the structure of atoms. He proposed that electrons can only occupy specific allowed orbits and can only emit or absorb radiation when jumping between them. Each jump has a fixed energy, giving a fixed frequency: ΔE = hν. This explained why hydrogen emits four specific wavelengths of visible light rather than a continuous spectrum.

Hydrogen Balmer Series · The Evidence for Quantized Energy
Hα 656 nm · n=3→2
Hβ 486 nm · n=4→2
Hγ 434 nm · n=5→2
Hδ 410 nm · n=6→2

Key Evidence

  • Hydrogen's emission spectrum: exactly four visible lines at 656, 486, 434, and 410 nm (Balmer series)
  • Bohr's formula En = −13.6/n² eV predicted all four frequencies with <1% error
  • The model explained atomic stability: no orbit has lower energy than n=1, so electrons cannot spiral further

Why It Was Replaced

Bohr's formula worked perfectly for hydrogen, one electron and one proton. It failed immediately for helium, which has two electrons. A model of atomic structure that only works for one out of 92 naturally occurring elements has a pretty serious problem.

Quantum Mechanical Model
Quantum Mechanical Model 1926

Schrödinger's wave equation treats electrons not as particles in fixed orbits but as probability distributions spread through space. The square of the wave function |ψ|² gives the probability of finding an electron at any given point. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle sets a hard limit: Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2. The more precisely you pin down position, the less you can know about momentum. This isn't a measurement problem. It's a feature of how nature works at small scales.

Electron Probability Density · 1s, 2s, and 2p Orbitals
1s 2s 2px

Key Evidence

  • de Broglie (1924): electrons have wavelength λ = h/mv; electron diffraction confirmed this experimentally
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2 (position and momentum cannot both be precisely known simultaneously)
  • Four quantum numbers (n, ℓ, mℓ, ms) specify each electron's state and forbid two electrons from sharing the same set (Pauli exclusion)

Current Status

This model is still correct. It accounts for atomic spectra, chemical bonding, periodic trends, and electron configurations. No experiment at atomic scales has contradicted it. Quantum field theory extends it to relativistic speeds and particle creation, but for chemistry, Schrödinger's equation holds.

Radioactive Decay

Nuclear instability, exponential mathematics, and the reliability that makes half-life the basis of geological dating. Decay follows first-order kinetics regardless of temperature, pressure, or chemical environment.

α

Alpha Decay

²³⁸92U→ ²³⁴90Th+ ⁴2He
  • Emitted particle: helium-4 nucleus (2p + 2n)
  • Mass number A decreases by 4
  • Atomic number Z decreases by 2
  • Penetrating power: very low (stopped by a sheet of paper or dead skin)
  • Ionizing power: very high, particularly dangerous if ingested or inhaled

Primary decay mode for heavy nuclei above bismuth (Z > 83). Uranium-238 and radium-226 both undergo alpha decay. The alpha particle is the helium-4 nucleus: one of the most tightly bound nuclear configurations, which is why it's emitted intact rather than as individual nucleons.

β

Beta Decay (β⁻)

¹⁴6C→ ¹⁴7N+ e⁻+ v̄e
  • A neutron converts to a proton: n → p + e⁻ + v̄e
  • Mass number A unchanged
  • Atomic number Z increases by 1
  • Penetrating power: medium (stopped by a few mm of aluminum)
  • Ionizing power: moderate

Carbon-14 undergoes β⁻ decay with a half-life of 5,730 years, becoming nitrogen-14. Cosmic rays continuously produce C-14 in the upper atmosphere, maintaining a steady atmospheric ratio. When an organism dies it stops exchanging carbon, and the C-14 clock starts. This is radiocarbon dating, reliable to about 50,000 years.

γ

Gamma Emission

⁶⁰27Co*→ ⁶⁰27Co+ γ
  • Emitted: high-energy electromagnetic photon
  • Mass number A: unchanged
  • Atomic number Z: unchanged
  • Penetrating power: extreme, needs centimeters of lead or meters of concrete to stop
  • Ionizing power: low per interaction, but deeply penetrating

Gamma emission typically follows alpha or beta decay when the daughter nucleus is left in an excited nuclear state (denoted with *). The nucleus de-excites by emitting a photon, just as an electron emits a photon when dropping to a lower energy level. Cobalt-60's gamma rays are used in radiation therapy for cancer treatment.

Matter at Every Scale

Intermolecular forces, states of matter, and phase transitions from cosmic plasma to molecular liquids. The same quantum mechanics that governs electron orbitals sets where water boils, which determines which moons can hold liquid on their surface.

States of Matter in Space

Space contains matter in all four states of aggregation, determined by local temperature and pressure.

PLASMA
Stellar interiors, solar wind, nebulae
Temperatures above ~104 K strip electrons from nuclei; matter is fully ionized
GAS
Nebulae, planetary atmospheres
H, He, CO, H₂O as gases in interstellar medium; molecules separated by vast distances
LIQUID
Europa, Enceladus, Titan
Liquid water under ice shells (tidal heating); liquid methane seas on Titan at −179°C
SOLID
Rocky planets, asteroids, neutron star crust
Silicate and metallic solids; neutron star crusts may be the densest solid matter in the universe

Intermolecular Forces

Molecular structure determines bulk physical properties through the forces molecules exert on each other. IMF strength sets boiling point, melting point, vapor pressure, viscosity, and solubility.

London Dispersion Weakest

Instantaneous dipoles from electron fluctuations. Present in all substances, polar or not. Strength increases with molar mass and molecular surface area, which explains why iodine (I₂, MW 254) is solid while fluorine (F₂, MW 38) is a gas at room temperature.

All molecules · strength ∝ polarizability · molar mass
Dipole-Dipole Moderate

Permanent partial charges on polar molecules attract each other. Present in addition to London dispersion in all polar substances. HCl boils 50°C higher than the nonpolar HBr of similar mass because of this.

Polar molecules: HCl, SO₂, acetone, CHCl₃
Hydrogen Bonding Strongest IMF

Hydrogen covalently bonded to N, O, or F forms an unusually strong electrostatic interaction, roughly 5–40 kJ/mol compared to 0.1–1 kJ/mol for London dispersion. DNA base pairing, protein secondary structure, and the anomalous properties of water all depend on H-bonds.

H covalently bonded to N, O, or F atoms

Phase Changes & Energy

Phase transitions require energy to break or form IMFs. The energy input doesn't change temperature during the transition. It goes entirely into structural reorganization.

PLASMA

kT ≫ IE; electrons completely stripped from nuclei; requires >104 K

↕+IE (ionization) / −IE (recombination)
GAS

IMFs fully overcome; random kinetic motion; pressure from molecular collisions

↕+ΔHvap (vaporization) / −ΔHvap (condensation)
LIQUID

Particles mobile; IMFs partially overcome; definite volume, no definite shape

↕+ΔHfus (melting) / −ΔHfus (freezing)
SOLID

Ordered lattice; particles vibrate about fixed positions; definite shape and volume

Why IMFs Determine Where Liquids Exist

The same forces you calculate in class determine which moons have oceans. Water's hydrogen bonds push its boiling point to 100°C. Methane's London dispersion only keeps it liquid below −161°C, which is why Titan's surface seas are methane, not water.

Where Elements Come From

All elements were produced by one of three processes. Bryson's "stardust" claim is literal: elements heavier than helium were made inside stars and scattered when those stars died.

Big Bang Nucleosynthesis
First 20 minutes after T = 0
¹H ⁴He ⁷Li
~75% H · ~25% He by mass

Only proton temperatures above 10⁹ K allow nuclear fusion. Cooling below that temperature in the first 20 minutes locked in these ratios permanently. All heavier elements required stars.

Stellar Nucleosynthesis
Billions of years of stellar evolution
CNO NeMgSi SCaFe
Elements up to iron (Z ≤ 26)

Shell burning in low- and high-mass stars builds elements from helium-burning (C, O) through silicon-burning (Fe, Ni). The iron peak on the binding energy curve marks the endpoint: no further energy can be extracted by fusion.

Elements heavier than iron (Z > 26)

Rapid neutron capture during supernovae and neutron-star mergers builds elements heavier than iron in seconds. Every gold atom on Earth was produced in one of these events and scattered through the galaxy over billions of years.

Energy as the Cosmic Thread

Energy runs through each section:

  • Big Bang: E = mc² converts pure energy to matter in the first second
  • Stellar fusion: mass defect releases binding energy as stars burn for billions of years
  • Nuclear decay: unstable nuclei shed energy as radiation, following ΔE = Δmc²
  • Phase changes: ΔHfus and ΔHvap are the energy cost of breaking IMFs
  • Emission spectra: ΔE = hν when electrons drop between energy levels

From nuclear scale to bulk matter, structure is predictive:

  • Neutron-to-proton ratio determines nuclear stability and decay mode
  • Electron configuration shapes periodic trends and reactivity
  • Molecular polarity and H-bonding capability set physical properties
  • Stellar mass sets the fusion pathway, core temperature, and which elements get made

How Atomic Models Were Revised

The atomic model timeline is SP-1 in practice. Each revision was forced by a specific experiment:

  • Dalton → Thomson: cathode rays proved atoms have internal structure
  • Thomson → Rutherford: gold foil backscattering proved charge is concentrated
  • Rutherford → Bohr: classical physics predicted atoms should collapse in nanoseconds
  • Bohr → Quantum: worked only for hydrogen; failed for multi-electron atoms
  • Quantum model: no experiment at atomic scales has contradicted it since 1926
